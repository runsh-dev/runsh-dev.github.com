Writing in the Margins

A note on restraint in design systems — “less” is not a slogan; it is the residue of every addition having been argued for.

Innei
2026·04·25 · Hangzhou

Every design system begins with a single judgment: what is necessary, and what is redundant. Yohaku proposes this — that beyond what you place on the canvas, the empty space is itself the content. This isn’t zen. It is engineering.

The split into three neutral tiers is the system’s most central judgment. Tier 1 (1–4) is reserved for surfaces. Tier 2 (5–7) carries borders and icons. Tier 3 (8–10) is where text lives. The contrast between tiers is opened up deliberately, so hierarchy reads from contrast alone — even before you check the value.

1Restraint is not subtraction

People tend to read restraint as “less.” That is the surface reading. Real restraint is this — every addition must pay the cost of being explained. When you must justify, in writing, why a new shade or a new button variant must exist, you stop at the necessary by yourself.

A thousand “no”s for every “yes.”

Accent is one shade of plum, #C56473. This isn’t a limit on color; it is color held in check. When attention is rare, every appearance carries weight.

2Why three tiers hold

The eye tops out around seven to nine distinguishable steps of neutral gray. The 50–950 schema is, deep down, a token system’s arrogance — the system can’t commit, so it hands the choice off to its users. Three tiers do the opposite. Each one means something specific.

  • Tier 1 (1–4) — surfaces, cards, fills
  • Tier 2 (5–7) — borders, icons, secondary text
  • Tier 3 (8–10) — body, headings, primary weight
